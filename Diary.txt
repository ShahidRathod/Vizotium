Grid Scaling Plan

## Initial Idea

Currently the grid square size is identical to the surface mesh square size. This creates a lower limit on how fine the visible grid can become.

One possible solution was to render a smaller grid inside each surface tile. By recursively subdividing tiles, arbitrarily fine grid resolutions could be achieved without increasing the underlying surface mesh resolution.

A future extension could be adaptive coordinates, where grid density is proportional to the local gradient magnitude at a point (x, y). Regions of high curvature or steep gradients would receive denser coordinate lines, while flatter regions would remain sparse.

[DO NOT IMPLEMENT ADAPTIVE COORDINATES. This is only a future research idea.]

I would also like the grid coordinate system to eventually become the coordinate grid of a general plane generated directly in the vertex shader.

## NEXT DAY — REVISED UNDERSTANDING

After further consideration, the original problem was misidentified.

(1) The objective is only to reduce the visual width/thickness of the grid lines.

(2) There is no practical reason to render surfaces with dimensions smaller than the underlying mesh resolution.

The current surface mesh is 200 × 200. This mesh represents the optimization landscape on which gradient trajectories will be visualized. Whether the trajectory computation occurs on the CPU or GPU, the mesh is already suitable for upload as a VBO.

For sensitive gradient optimization visualizations, resolutions significantly below 200 × 200 do not appear to provide sufficient accuracy. Therefore, the full 200 × 200 surface should always be rendered.

## Conclusion

The idea of introducing sub-grids inside individual mesh cells is unnecessary.

Even a 100 × 100 visible grid is already visually dense. The requirement is not higher grid density, but thinner grid rendering.

The mesh resolution remains unchanged; only the visual appearance of the grid lines should be adjusted.

Persistence is usually defined as continuing despite difficulty. But the deeper meaning is more subtle.

Persistence is maintaining a long-term objective while repeatedly updating the short-term path.

People often confuse persistence with stubbornness. They are different:

Persistence: The goal stays fixed; the method changes when evidence shows a better approach.
Stubbornness: The method stays fixed even when evidence shows it isn't working.

At a deeper level, persistence has several dimensions:

Persistence against discomfort
Continuing when you're bored, tired, or unmotivated.
Example: studying for two hours even when you don't feel like it.
Persistence against failure
Treating failures as information rather than as verdicts.
Each failure updates your model of reality.
Persistence against time
Continuing when progress is invisible.
Many complex skills (mathematics, programming, languages) have long periods where improvement isn't obvious.
Persistence of identity
Acting consistently with the person you intend to become.
"I practice because I'm becoming an engineer," not because today's session is enjoyable.

From a systems perspective, persistence is the ability to continue producing useful work despite disturbances. In control theory, you'd call this robustness. In optimization, it's continuing to search for a minimum despite local setbacks. In biology, evolution itself is a form of persistence across generations.

A useful way to think about it is:

Persistence is not refusing to quit.

It is refusing to let temporary conditions determine permanent decisions.

Notice that this doesn't mean never quitting. Sometimes the persistent choice is to abandon a strategy that isn't working and adopt a better one. The objective persists; the implementation evolves.

For someone building difficult technical skills—such as graphics programming or advanced C++—persistence often looks mundane rather than dramatic:

writing code every day,
debugging the same problem for hours,
reading documentation instead of searching for shortcuts,
accepting that mastery accumulates from thousands of small corrections.

In that sense, persistence is less about willpower and more about consistently investing effort over long timescales while adapting to new evidence.
